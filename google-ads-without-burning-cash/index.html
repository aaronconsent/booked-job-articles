Google Ads Without Lighting Money on Fire

AP By Aaron Phillips · Marketing 101 · Lesson 8 of 10 · Updated June 2026
Short answer: Search ads put you in front of someone searching for your trade right now. The fix to not getting burned: answer every call, start tiny with tight keywords, track by cost per booked job, kill the losers.

Of every door in this course, paid ads is where contractors light money on fire the fastest. Thousands of dollars, gone, with nothing to show but a pretty report. But done right, that first ad dollar can come back with friends. So let's spend it like adults — without torching it.

$200
the size of the job you set on fire every time a paid click rings your phone and nobody answers. Fix the phone before you spend a dime.

Search Ads = Catching Intent

search ads = caught at "near me", right now

We're talking search ads — the ones at the top of Google. And here's why they beat a billboard. A billboard hopes somebody needs you someday. A search ad puts you in front of a guy who is typing emergency plumber near me, right now, with water on the floor. You're not interrupting him. You're answering the door he just knocked on. That's the highest intent there is.

Where The Money Bleeds

bleeds → broad keywords · no tracking · slow callbacks

Now, a click in the home trades runs you about six, seven bucks, sometimes a lot more. And the money bleeds out three ways. One, broad keywords — you pay six bucks for some kid Googling how to fix a toilet himself. Two, no tracking, so you have no clue what worked. And three, slow callbacks, which we'll get to, because that one's a killer.

The Missed-Call Trap

paid click + missed call = $200 on fire

Here's the one that makes me crazy. You pay six, seven dollars for the click. The ad works. The phone rings. And nobody picks up. You just paid for a lead and threw it in the trash. A missed call on a paid ad isn't a missed call — it's a two-hundred-dollar job you set on fire. The ad did its job. You didn't.

Start Tiny

small budget · tight keywords · one city · WATCH it

So how do you not get burned? You start tiny. Do not dump five grand in on day one. Set a small daily budget you won't cry over. Use tight keywords — emergency plumber, not just plumbing. Pick one city. And then you actually watch it, like it's a space heater near the curtains. Small, tight, and watched.

Track & Kill

track by CPBJ → feed winners, KILL losers

Then you track everything, and you judge it by the same number as always — cost per booked job. Not clicks, not calls. Booked jobs. Some keywords will print money. Most will just spend it. Find the few winners, pour the budget into those, and kill the losers without mercy. That's the whole game — feed what works, starve what doesn't.

Do This Now

ANSWER THE PHONE before you spend a dime

And here's the homework, and it comes before you spend a single dollar. Make sure every call gets answered. By you, by a real person, or by a service that picks up. Because running ads to a phone nobody answers is the dumbest money in this entire course. Fix the phone first. Then, and only then, turn on the ads.

Frequently asked questions

How much should a contractor spend on Google Ads to start?

Start tiny — a small daily budget you won't cry over, tight keywords (e.g. 'emergency plumber', not 'plumbing'), one city, and watch it daily.

Why are my Google Ads not working?

Usually three leaks: broad keywords paying for tire-kickers, no tracking, and slow or missed callbacks. A missed call on a paid ad is money set on fire.

How do I measure if my ads are profitable?

By cost per booked job, not clicks or calls. Find the few keywords that book real work, feed those, and kill the rest without mercy.
